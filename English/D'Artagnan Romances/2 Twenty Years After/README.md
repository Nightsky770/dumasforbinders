* I have added an explanatory footnote to the use of "tutoyer" in chapter 12. As French students everywhere know, French has two pronouns used to talk to another person: "tu", for intimates and social inferiors (plus some special contexts like insults); and "vous", for social equals and superiors. "Tutoyer" means "to use 'tu' to speak with". (There is, naturally, also "vouvoyer", "to use 'vous' to speak with".) Since modern English lacks formal and informal pronouns, there's no word (or concept, really) equivalent to "tutoyer" and the original translator just left it in quotes. While an extremely careful reader might notice that D'Artagnan refers to Mousqueton with "thou/thee" before this conversation, and "you" after, and (correctly) deduce that "tutoyer" means "to use informal pronouns with someone", it's not at all clear and I thought I'd clarify matters. I'd have translated it differently.
